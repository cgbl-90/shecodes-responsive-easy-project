Mangu History

Like the African fufu and Puerto Rican mofongo, the Ecuadorian bolón de verde and other similar dishes, the mangú is a version of the West African fufu.
It arrived in the Caribbean with the slaves of ewe, fon, wolof, ashanti, Yoruba or dinka origin. In Africa, this dish is made mainly with tubers, mainly cassava and yams, cooked and then crushed in a large mortar until a paste is obtained, fufu, which is usually served accompanied by a fish soup, tomato and ground peanut.
Among the West African slaves employed in the plantations of the Caribbean and the north Pacific of South America, this dish began to be made with cooked bananas, reserving the tubers for more substantial meals such as sancocho, while it used to be accompanied by the leftovers of the white masters.
With the emancipation of the black slaves, leftovers were replaced by meat ingredients - such as cold cuts or dried meat -, cheese and fried eggs; due to its simplicity and its high nutritional value, this dish transcended racial and social barriers, becoming a very popular meal for all social classes.
According to a popular legend, the word "mangú" comes from the time of the first American invasion (1916-1924) to the Dominican Republic; when tasting this mashed bananas, they expressed: "Man, (this is) good!", (man, this is good!) and the Dominicans, not mastering English, took the expression to name this dish and continued to use the term. But this is something of which there is no evidence and it is more a folk tale. It is much more likely that the word is a variation of the word "mangusi", a name used in some parts of Congo for any tuber that is boiled and consumed in mashed form
